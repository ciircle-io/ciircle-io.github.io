The Vow and the Principles

The Vow

What exactly is this vow?

The vow is simply a promise you make to yourself.

You only make it to yourself. You do not make it to any other person, organization, or entity. You can keep it secret or make it public. It's entirely up to you.

The vow does not need to replace or supersede any other promise, vow, or oath you have made. Again, it is entirely up to you.

The Principles

What are the principles?

The principles are humility, compassion, clarity, and generosity.

You make the oath to yourself, so you are the final arbiter of what these principles mean. There is no official definition of these principles, but I offer a few personal thoughts below.

humility

Humility is simply seeking to be honest with oneself.

Most importantly, this means accepting that I do not know everything, even about myself. I am always changing and the world around me is always changing. Only if I accept that there is potential for something new in me and in my view of the world can I embrace who I am.

Humility should not be confused with humiliation. To submit to humiliation is to accept another's definition of who we are. That is not being honest with oneself, because no external person or group can know the rich and deep depths of who I am.

compassion

Compassion is being open to understanding the feelings and experiences of others.

I think it is useful to contrast compassion with empathy. Compassion uses the root "com-" which means "with." Empathy uses the root "em-" which means "in". Empathy generally implies that we have the experience to understand someone else's feelings. Empathy uses personal experience to analogize the pain and suffering of others.

In contrast, I think of compassion as trying to experience something with someone, not for them. I listen to them. I accept what they are feeling as something they are feeling, and not as what I think they should feel or what I might feel in their place.

So if someone tells me they are hurt, my empathetic response might be to say, "I share your hurt." In contrast, my compassionate response might be, "I hear that you are hurt. It hurts me that you are hurt. I know I can't take away your hurt, but I want to understand your hurt, so please tell me how and why you are hurt."

clarity

Clarity is removing obstacles to reach a deeper understanding of the truth of things.

I think it is important to acknowledge that truth is neither absolute nor is it relative. It is directional. There is no final truth, but there are better and worse understandings of truth.

generosity

Generosity is offering what we have to others.
